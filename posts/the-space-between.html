The Space Between: On Folding, Symmetries and Sampling

Folding as a way to study symmetries learned by neural representations.

Driving Question

This work asks what folding can tell us about symmetries in data. If two inputs are different but should be treated similarly, we can write that relationship informally as \(x\sim x'\).

A representation that respects this relation should preserve what matters and ignore what does not. In geometric terms, the network may bring related inputs closer together in its internal space:

\[ d_{\mathcal{Z}}(f(x), f(x')) \ll d_{\mathcal{X}}(x,x'). \]

The paper extends how folding is measured and emphasizes the practical problem of sampling: the measured geometry depends on which paths or points we choose to inspect.

In that sense, folding becomes a way to ask what structure a network merges, preserves, or separates internally.

This workshop paper was later expanded into Exploiting Space Folding by Neural Networks, which develops the training objective and the adversarial-trajectory analysis in greater detail.
