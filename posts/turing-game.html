The Turing Game

A public, gamified experiment for studying whether people can distinguish human chat partners from AI chatbots.

Driving Question

The Turing Game is an interactive implementation of Turing's original imitation game. It turns the question into an online interaction: two human players and one AI chatbot enter a chat setting, and the humans try to identify who is human and who is machine.

Multiplayer LLM Interaction

Much of conversational AI evaluation is dyadic: one user prompts one model, and the model is judged by answer quality, factuality, or stylistic plausibility. Multiplayer interaction probes a different layer of behavior. In a shared conversation, an LLM must respond not only to a prompt, but also to turn-taking, changing common ground, implicit alliances, suspicion, and strategic ambiguity.

Games have become testbeds for language-model agents. Work on Diplomacy combines natural-language negotiation with strategic coordination; generative-agent environments study believable social behavior in simulated communities; and text-based social-deduction settings such as AmongAgents examine reasoning under incomplete information, deception, and group deliberation.

The Turing Game connects this direction back to the imitation-game question. Its three-player format embeds one model among two humans, so the central measurement is not only whether a single answer sounds human, but whether the model remains plausible under multi-party scrutiny while the human players coordinate to identify it.

The experiment asks whether current language models can pass as human in a conversation and collects structured interaction data: where do humans notice machine-like behavior, and where do large language models become hard to distinguish from human interlocutors?

The implementation is gamified because that makes the experimental setting easier to scale. It lets people participate naturally, while the system records the decisions, conversations, and interaction patterns needed for analysis.

The paper reports early results from a public launch at Ars Electronica Festival in September 2024. The longer-term aim is to better understand human-AI interaction and use that understanding to improve language-based interfaces.
